# Medication Trial History

*Clinical record of medications tried, outcomes, and mechanistic interpretation.*
*Last updated: 2026-04-30*

## Clonazepam (benzodiazepine)

**Mechanism:** GABA-A receptor potentiator

| | |
|---|---|
| Week 1 (lower dose) | Helpful |
| Week 2 (higher dose) | Episodes got worse; side effects severe |
| Classification | Paradoxical benzo reaction |

**What happened:** At higher dose, episodes worsened — not just side effects. A paradoxical benzo reaction. At higher doses, GABA-A potentiation preferentially sedates supraspinal circuits before spinal ones, which can produce descending disinhibition and increase spinal excitability. The result is more motor activity, not less.

**What this tells us:** GABA-A flooding is not the right mechanism for this presentation. The inhibitory circuit dysfunction is real (low-dose clonazepam helped), but GABA-A is not a scalable lever. Paradoxical benzo reactions are more common in autistic individuals and in patients with existing CNS inhibitory circuit dysfunction.

**Implications for future treatment:**
- Do not escalate GABA-A agents
- Glycinergic pathway (THC target) is the confirmed effective mechanism
- GABA-B (baclofen) operates on a different receptor and avoids this risk
- Keppra (SV2A / glycine-GABA rescue) does not flood GABA-A directly — a better mechanistic fit

## THC (cannabinoid)

**Mechanism:** Glycine receptor potentiation + CB1/GABA modulation + mast cell stabilization + descending pain modulation (PAG)

| | |
|---|---|
| Dose | 2–3mg sublingual |
| Outcome | Reliably aborts active episodes and stops pain |
| Use | Abortive (taken during active episode) |

**Significance:** Confirms that spinal glycinergic circuits are the correct pharmacological target. This is the clearest pharmacodynamic signal in the dataset.

## Keppra (levetiracetam) — recommended 2026-04-30, not yet tried

**Recommending physician:** Autoimmune specialist

**Mechanism:** SV2A binding → rescues glycine and GABA receptor function in spinal neurons; does not directly flood GABA-A

**Mechanistic fit:** Strong — SV2A/glycine rescue converges with THC's glycinergic mechanism from a different upstream point. Spinal cord specificity (EC50 0.7µM in spinal neurons) maps directly to the bottom-up propagation pattern.

**Evidence base for this presentation:**
- Spinal myoclonus: case-report level, positive (2002 Keswani et al., *JNNP*)
- SPS-spectrum: case reports + small series, 1000–2000mg/day, paroxysmal symptom reduction (Rüegg et al., 2004 *Neurology*); listed as add-on in 2025 systematic review
- Best evidence overall is for cortical/epileptic myoclonus — spinal evidence is limited but mechanistically coherent

**Risk flags specific to this patient:**
- MCAS: formal hypersensitivity/anaphylaxis risk — test-dose protocol warranted; check excipients
- Autistic adult: behavioral side effects (irritability, agitation) ~2.3× higher in people with developmental differences; autistic regression documented in one case report (reversible)
- Fatigue: additive with existing non-restorative sleep and post-episodic weakness
- Autonomic: QT monitoring reasonable given dysautonomia baseline

**THC interaction:** No pharmacokinetic interaction (Keppra is renally cleared, no CYP). Modest additive sedation pharmacodynamically. Mechanistic convergence may allow lower doses of each.

**Mitigation strategy:**
- Start at lowest available dose, titrate slowly
- Pyridoxine (B6) 50–100mg/day prophylactically from day 1 (shown to reduce behavioral side effects; low-risk co-administration)
- Agreed stopping rule before initiation
- Explicit behavioral tracking against baseline during first month

## Key talking points for specialist appointments

1. Clonazepam produced a paradoxical reaction at higher dose — episodes worsened, not just side effects. This suggests GABA-A flooding is contraindicated at higher doses.
2. THC reliably aborts episodes — confirmed glycinergic mechanism. Spinal glycine circuit is the correct target.
3. These two data points together argue for Keppra or baclofen over further benzodiazepine trials.
4. Given MCAS, request a test-dose protocol for any new medication.
5. Ask about pyridoxine co-administration with Keppra given autistic adult profile.
6. Ask to compare Keppra vs. pregabalin given the pain + sleep + RLS burden.
